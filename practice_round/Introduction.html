As stated before, there are two roles in this experiment: client and expert. You have been randomly selected to be an expert. There are two types of expert. A high-ability expert, and a low-ability expert. The probability that an expert is a high-ability expert is x% and the probability that an expert is a low-ability expert is y%. These probabilities are common knowledge, and clients also know them. You have been randomly assigned to be a x expert. You keep that role for the entire experiment. All clients can observe your ability level. The experiment consists of 25 rounds. In total, there are 3 clients and 3 experts.

Each round follows the same basic sequence of decisions. Be mindful that you need to understand both roles in order to answer the comprehension questions and make sensible decisions that determine your final bonus payoff.

Each round, clients receive either a big problem with a probability of x%, or a small problem with a probability of y%. Clients will never be informed about which problem they actually have. In order to solve the problem, a client needs to approach an expert. Alternatively, each client can also choose not to enter the market, and avoid interaction with an expert. If a client decides to enter the market, he or she can choose one of z experts. If the chosen expert successfully solves the clientâ€™s problem, the client receives 150 Coins for that round. If the problem is not solved, the client does not receive anything. Irrespective of the outcome, the client has to pay the price chosen by the expert. All prices by each expert and their respective ability level are visible to the client when deciding which expert to approach.
